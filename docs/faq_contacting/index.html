FAQs

Contacting sellers

  • Not necessarily.

    Early market research can involve more than the existing sellers you have on any one panel.  The idea is to discover new trends, new technologies, innovation and new sellers, including international offerings. That is not to say, however, that existing sellers and suppliers can’t offer you services in a new way or in a new area, so it’s likely that you will involve existing suppliers, but there is no rule that they have to be involved and consulted.

  • A tip to get you started is to always approach the open market in your early market research as this avoids missing out on those sellers you don’t even know about — you might think you know the market and who the leading sellers are but this is not necessarily the case. There may be companies that are just starting, with a huge potential, that you have never heard about.

    Another approach is to consider the scale, scope and sensitivity of your activity, take some time to identity and document the related risks, engage with appropriate internal stakeholders and document some initial desktop research.  This can assist in navigating to a defensible list of potential suppliers which you would like to pursue further discussions with or guide you to a wider/open approach.

  • You can find information about potential vendors from these sources: 

    • You can always start with internet research to get a general idea of who the players in the market are
    • Look for seller names on AusTender (or a state government equivalent, for example, QTenders, NSW eTenders, etc.), then go to their website for more information
    • Industry and other research and data holding bodies (for example: Gartner or IBISWorld)
    • Existing panels, such as the DTA Digital Marketplace
    • Previous procurement and agency records and documents – Both internally and across government
    • Talk to stakeholders that have conducted similar research or procurement

    There may be an internal approval process before you start making phone calls, or sending out a Brief, so be sure to check your agency’s internal policy or have a chat with your souring team.

  • After receiving submissions from the open market, shortlist sellers you really want to talk to. This is done based on some pre-determined criteria that will be specific to the digital sourcing project, so check with your teams if you are uncertain. You may like to use targeted emails, or phone conversations to shortlist vendors.

    Remember to document your activity.

  • Much of your early market research may just be just that — research — or you may also find it useful to contact and connect with sellers. Here are some ideas on how you can engage with sellers in your research:

    Engagement:

    • Much of this early engagement will be informal, and should involve a gradual learning journey that progressively builds knowledge from your research phase:
    • Attendance at industry conferences and trade shows
    • Seller and Supplier visits/tours
    • Product demonstrations and site/facilities inspections
    • Inviting industry to participate in working groups
    • Industry briefings or ‘consultation days’ or ‘technology days’
    • Engagement through any industry forums or groups
    • Engagement of advisers or other industry experts
    • Use of industry stakeholder or working groups to review and provide feedback on potential solutions, options or requirements

  • After you have shortlisted your sellers, you should end up with an ‘evaluation outcome’ document of some sort; it can be an Evaluation Report, or just a list of sellers shortlisted in an Excel sheet (see the Documents & Templates section for options). 

    Make sure you document:

    • your decision-making process and application of the assessment criteria
    • who ran the shortlisting
    • the final list of sellers, with their contact details 

    There is also a need to justify the reasons for NOT shortlisting potential sellers (possible complaints, situations when a seller contacts you later and asks why they were not invited, etc.).

  • It’s a good idea to publish a Brief that:

    • states the problem
    • says what you are looking for
    • sets out any pre-determined criteria for the solution (this can be adapted as you progress through your research)
    • says how you are going to engage with the market
    • details how you are going to shortlist sellers

    Be honest and don’t mislead suppliers. If your department is not going to do anything after the early market research (apart from issuing an internal report), tell this to sellers; they will then understand that there is no sales opportunity (and it will help them to stop pestering you).

    Note that you should treat all sellers the same in your early market research, as differences in treatment would contravene fairness and transparency principles and undermine confidence in the digital sourcing process.
