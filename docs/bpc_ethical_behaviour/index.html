Ethical behaviour

Question:

It makes me worried about undertaking such research again, as I know there’s a growing awareness of unethical behaviour and I want to be able to respond confidently to these types of complaints.

Answer:

This is such a commonly asked question — ethical behaviour is probably the issue that many people new to early market research get nervous about, wondering … “Will it look bad if I go for a coffee with a seller?” or “How will I deal with complaints from sellers saying ‘Why didn’t you contact me?!’” as you’ve pointed out.

Here are some ideas to help you with your next project.

  • There could be a seller who feels they have not been treated fairly and may choose to make a complaint. We can prepare for such complaints so that if they occur, we’re ready with the relevant documentation to respond to them appropriately, with established non-discriminatory complaint-handling procedures and in a timely manner.

    So, what do you need to do? Here are our tips:

    1. Make sure you document every step of your early market research, especially why certain suppliers were contacted, and why certain suppliers were not contacted. Obviously you don’t have to do this for every single seller you don’t contact, but having a range in your documents will help to show that you have cast a wide net and considered as many as you were able, commensurate with the scale, scope and risks of the activity.
    2. Be transparent with sellers (we'll look at this more in the next Q and A).
    3. Check over your agency’s complaint-handling procedures to see if there are any other agency documents to help you log your process/activities and be prepared for any potentially unhappy suppliers.
    4. Talk to your procurement team before you start your early market research to see what advice they can offer about planning for complaints.

  • In your early market research, you need to recognise and deal with actual, potential and perceived conflicts of interest. A conflict of interest occurs when in the execution of our duties as government employees, we find ourselves in a position to be potentially influenced, or appear to be influenced, by a private or personal interest, that could lead us to try to gain a personal advantage or to avoid a personal disadvantage.

    So, how can we manage this? Here are three tips:

    1. Processes

      The best way to handle a conflict of interest is to already have a process in place to manage it – and your agency should have one in place. That means documenting any potential conflict (real or perceived) that may happen and deciding how it should be handled and who should be involved in dealing with it.
      A potential conflict of interest does not mean the activity needs to be scrapped.  There are basically 3 ways to deal with an identified real, potential or perceived conflict of interest:

      • Limit involvement of identified people to control and document any conflict,
      • Remove involvement of identified people if it is determined that any involvement is too risky, or
      • Determine there is no conflict and document the rationale.

      Talk to your procurement team about their conflict-of-interest processes.

    2. Declarations

      Ensure everyone involved is made aware of the activity, the parties involved and the scope.

      Ensure everyone involved makes a Conflict of Interest Declaration – Even if they have nothing to declare.

      This might include acknowledging that you know or have worked with a seller or have shares or other interests in a particular company.

    3. Transparency and Documentation

      Get any potential conflict of interest out into the open, up font, prior to the activity commencing.

      Ensure you document any and all identified real, potential or perceived conflicts of interest and declarations.  A good practice is to also maintain a central ‘Probity Register’, which, among other things, documents declarations and how each are being treated.

  • There are two key aspects to dealing with potential suppliers equitably:

    1. Seeking appropriate internal or external advice when probity issues arise

      It is better practice to appoint a probity officer when engaging with the market. For large scale or risky activities, many agencies opt to engage an external probity officer to assist with their early market research. This person will be involved throughout the entire process and will help to provide advice to you to ensure your actions and communications are equitable. They are also helpful for any complaints that may arise from unhappy sellers. Talk to your procurement team if you’d like to engage a probity advisor during your early market research.

    2. Not accepting inappropriate gifts or hospitality

      This is sometimes viewed as a tricky one — what does ‘hospitality’ mean?

      Your agency will have clear guidelines on accepting hospitality – But when in doubt, don’t.

      But by way of example, take a look at the scenarios below.

  • Imagine you are responsible for the early market research for Project ABC. You meet with most sellers in your office; however, one provider asks if you would like to meet at their head office location to talk about the project over a light lunch.

    What should you do?

    The offered hospitality is likely of minimal monetary value. However, it would not be appropriate for you to accept this offer, as it could be seen (‘perceived’) as an enticement that could influence your decision to move the procurement forward with this seller. You should politely decline the offer and arrange to meet the potential provider in your office.

  • You are starting your early market research process and have found that a face-to-face coffee chat with sellers is working well to share information. The seller usually pays for these coffees, and sometimes there are follow-up coffee meetings.

    Is there any issue here?

    Accepting one coffee is unlikely to cause a conflict of interest in how you carry out your duties. However, receiving regular coffees can quickly amount to considerable monetary value, which may be perceived as influencing how you deal with these suppliers. Ideally, you should ensure that you pay for your own coffee at every meeting, and ensure you spend equitable time with all the suppliers on your shortlist.

    You must refuse bribes, ‘facilitation payments’ or any other enticements that could be perceived as offering you personal gain. Report all attempts to your manager, probity advisor and/or your procurement team – Even if the offer was made in innocently; it is better to have it reported and documented, than have it ‘come out later’ and cause potential issues.
