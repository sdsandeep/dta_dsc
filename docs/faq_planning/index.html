FAQs

Planning and preparation

  • Not all needs require early market research, but if you can answer ‘yes’ to any one of these questions, then you should consider it: 

    • Is there doubt over the existence of a market for the identified business need or product? 
    • Assuming there is a market of some kind, are there doubts over its capacity, capability, maturity or competitiveness?
    • Is first-hand in-house agency knowledge of the market superficial, incomplete or simply absent?
    • Is there uncertainty about the level of seller interest in your outcome? 
    • Is the desired outcome likely to involve significant business change within your agency?
    • Is the level of expected business change within the agency exceptional?
    • Is there a need to manage expectations of the program with sellers?
    • Is your agency requirement unusual or novel?
    • Is the solution to your requirement likely to be complex or innovative? 

    Early market research is not appropriate for every situation, but where it is, it should begin at the earliest possible stage in the digital sourcing process to assess the reaction of the market, the proposal required, and the digital sourcing approach. 

  • Your agency should not undertake early market research if:

    the agency has an existing standing offer arrangement (SOA) in place that continues to offer value for money

    there are other sensitivities around the program in question (e.g. security or political)

  • Panels are a great resource for early market research, especially with panels that are all encompassing, for example, the ‘Ask the Market’ option in the Digital Market Place can reach over 1200 sellers, so panels should certainly be considered. Panels may also assist in framing the appropriate sellers to approach to inform your activity.

    One thing to consider, however, is that if your requirement is unusual or novel, panels may overlook emerging or new sellers, or smaller enterprises who haven’t applied to be on a panel yet. If we are to invite innovative solutions, then doing early market research both with panels, and outside of panels, will give us the best options to find something that suits our needs, offers value for money and can future-proof projects with the most up-to-date solutions and technology.

  • By talking to industry early in the digital sourcing processes, we give them time to meet the future demands of government.

    It provides an opportunity for industry to be informed about government digital sourcing processes, which is particularly relevant in sectors where suppliers may lack experience in completing government tenders.

    We don’t know what we don’t know! By conducting early market research, sellers are able to better inform us of the industry capabilities, emerging technologies and new solutions.

    Complex, innovative or high-risk programs can be adapted to better utilise industry capacities and position sellers to better meet the needs of such programs.

    Early market research can help shape the requirement so that digital sourcing is either focused on what the market can deliver, or helps to shape the activity to understand that there may be some development required in order to achieve the outcome. This also greatly assists in meeting internal stakeholder expectations around budget and cost.

    Through this research, we also educate the market and confront any preconceptions regarding transacting with agencies.

    Early market research is outcomes focussed — that means we acknowledge that we are not the experts, and we let the market tell us what’s possible instead of working in a vacuum.

    For some examples of early market research, check out our Case Studies section.
