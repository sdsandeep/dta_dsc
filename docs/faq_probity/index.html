Probity

  • Probity is the complete and confirmed integrity, uprightness and honesty in a digital sourcing or pre-digital sourcing process. In short, it is the evidence of ethical behaviour.

    As government agencies, we have an obligation to ensure our digital sourcing conduct is always fair, ethical, transparent and probity-rich. If we have clear, visible and meaningful commitments to fairness, then it encourages sellers to want to do business with us - now and in the future.

  • Probity risks in early market engagement include:

    • not treating sellers equitably
    • claims of bias
    • claims of improper use of position
    • accepting hospitality, gifts of benefits from sellers
    • conflicts of interest (real or perceived)
    • excluding sellers for trivial, indefensible reasons

    Whatever method is chosen to undertake early market research, it is critical that there is no element of bid selection, evaluation, actual or perceived conflict of interest, commitment or undertaking towards any potential seller.

    Where information is provided by a seller, and an agency wishes to use that information, careful consideration must be given to potentially confidential information, or use of the seller’s intellectual property.

  • It is important to note that the obligations of probity and accountability should in no way stop an agency from undertaking early market research and engaging in reasonable discussion with potential suppliers for a new digital sourcing requirement. Rather, early market research gives us an opportunity to demonstrate to industry that we are undertaking due diligence and consultation before presenting a formal request to the market.

    Probity decisions should be:

    • Helpful: Probity should be used to facilitate discussion of current approaches to market with suppliers in order to promote genuine engagement.
    • Inclusive: Probity processes should be designed to enable innovative approaches to be adopted if required.
    • Tailored: Probity roles can be tailored to the business need and range from using internal expertise through to engaging external specialist advice.
    • Sensible: Each individual process may require a different approach; one size will not fit all.

    Read over the case studies and better practice corner posts to find out more about how you can show evidence of ethical behaviour in your research.

  • It’s often thought that meeting sellers for an informal chat over coffee might be cause probity issues, but as long as you tell the seller you will report the meeting to your manager (and you follow through with that) and make a record of the meeting, you’ll have protected yourself and the research. Also ensure you reiterate that you are in the early market research phase and don’t guarantee work whatsoever.

    It is also best if you pay for your own coffee.

    Note that receiving unsolicited calls are also fine, you just need to document the call, including the outcome as a file note.
