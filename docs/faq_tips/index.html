Tips for success

  • External resources can offer specialist experience and knowledge that you or your agency may not currently possess.  You may want to involve an external resource in anything, from offering advice to managing the whole early market research process.

    Here are some things to consider when using an external resource for this early stage of industry research:

    • External resources can only advise you on what they see as the best options, based on the information they hold and what they are told by the agency and industry.
    • The business risk of the program as a whole remains with the agency commissioning the resource.
    • External resources who offer advice, or who manage the process on behalf of the agency, must be impartial.
    • You and your staff must learn from the external resource to avoid reliance upon them (knowledge transfer).
    • Engage with the seller base as early as possible. Before consultation happens, make sure you do research to establish whether any new sellers have emerged since any previous digital sourcing or market research activities were undertaken (remember it is a fast paced, dynamic landscape out there).
    • Consulting with the broadest range of sellers possible, helps ensure that a range of different perspectives are considered.
    • Make sure sellers know you are in early market research phase and don’t guarantee work – make this very clear.
    • Discussions should centre on the ‘ends’ rather than the ‘means’ (define the outcome you are seeking, not what you think the requirements are).
    • Gather information rather than providing details of the desired solution, and try and stay focused on the desired outcomes, while allowing industry to demonstrate how they could help achieve those outcomes.
    • The most common issue is usually a lack of planning. This leads to prioritising schedule above all else and then specifying a solution to save time.
    • Keep options open and avoid the tendency to 'zero in' on particular options — allow sellers the freedom to suggest ways forward.
    • Make sure your communication with the seller base is as uniform as possible. You should ensure that all identified sellers are given the same level of access to digital sourcing staff and the same level of opportunity to contribute to the engagement process. You also need to ensure you document your activity and engagement.
    • Meeting with seller representatives in person is the most straightforward, and often the most successful method of engaging with industry. Individual meetings with sellers can often lead to more useful information being elicited over brining larger groups together and giving a briefing, as sellers are likely to be reluctant to share information when competitors are present.
    • You can discuss previous digital sourcing for the same products/services or in the same market space. This will assist sellers in providing relevant information and insights. However, be mindful of not disclosing commercial-in-confidence information, or getting side-tracked into a discussion on past contracts, rather than future opportunities.

  • It is important to be as informed as possible about the market before commencing the engagement. This will make it possible for the agency to determine what they do and don’t know, and the subsequent market research can be used to fill in the knowledge gaps.

    Sound recordkeeping is vital to document that an agency’s probity and accountability obligations have been adhered to. Records of your early market research should include who the agency has engaged with, the reason for the engagement, the activities conducted and any actions resulting from the engagement.

  • Proper planning is necessary if early market research is to result in the desired outcome.

    Poorly planned early market research can result in inadequate outcomes for both your agency and industry, as well as unjustified costs in terms of time spent and resources allocated to the engagement. Your agency could also suffer from reputational damage if industry feels that the early market research process was poorly facilitated.

    There is no ‘one size fits all’ approach to planning an early market research process. It is important to understand what information you want to share with industry, what information is required from industry, and what questions need to be raised, before initiating contact. This will help ensure that the agency drives the direction of the engagement.

    If industry sets the parameters of the discussion, there is a risk that the information attained is akin to a ‘sales pitch’, which may not be beneficial in terms of broadening the knowledge of industry or clarifying your requirements.

  • Risks associated with a lack of early market research include:

    • poorly designed specifications
    • unsuitable conditions of contract
    • a lack of tender responses
    • inferior value-for-money outcomes

  • The early market research process itself carries little risk to you or your agency if these principles are followed:

    • Uniformity: while no two sellers are alike, engagement with all sellers consulted should be as uniform and consistent as possible.
    • Security: if information is provided by a seller, it should not be shared with other sellers (directly or inadvertently) or people outside of your agency unless expressed permission from the seller is attained. Any documentation provided by sellers should be stored securely.
    • Boundaries: you and your staff should be open with sellers about what can and cannot be discussed. The purpose of the early market research must be clearly communicated to sellers in the Brief to avoid any misunderstanding.

  • Early market research is considered to be a planning activity - the process should cease when the your agency has understood what it is that is required and you are prepared to proceed to an Approach to Market (or have determined that the digital sourcing process will not proceed).

    Communications to sellers about the end of the early market research period should be made at the same time, to ensure that no seller is unfairly advantaged.

    It is important to note that ending the early market research period should not inhibit standard ‘business as usual’ with existing suppliers. However, once the digital sourcing becomes a competitive process, any discussion that could potentially advantage (whether real or perceived) one seller over another should not occur.
