Case Studies

We’ve spoken to some digital sourcing specialists in different government agencies to gather their stories of early market research and engagement. Through these, you can gain ideas on when early market research has been done by others, and how their processes and experiences may help you with your own agency research.

If you would like to submit your own fabulous case study for the benefit of others, please contact dsn@dta.gov.au.

The Great Australian Bite (GAB)
Marine Survey 2 procurement

This is in relation to the Great Australian Bite (GAB) Marine Survey 2 procurement conducted by the Commonwealth Science and Industrial Research Organisation (CSIRO). The project aimed to research natural resources available in the GAB to a depth of 3000 meters below sea level. The final value of the procurement was $7.3m.

The business unit (GAB Project team) undertook limited market research and proposed to approach only one provider to undertake the work.

CSIRO scientists have a strong understanding of this type of project as it is highly specialised. They received specific external funding to undertake this type of research. Often, they draw on past experience or the knowledge of other scientists in CSIRO to find a solution. This can lead to limited tender procurements, avoiding open tender processes and limiting innovation and competition in the market.

As this was a procurement of a charter of a research vessel, the market is limited but providers are available internationally (e.g. Singapore, US, Europe). The business unit was seeking to engage a provider using exemption 4 of the CPR’s – “procurements funded by grants and sponsorship payments from non-Commonwealth entities” – as the GAB Survey was funded by an external partner.

There were several concerns with this approach, including: 

  • once the funding for the project ($20m) was transferred to CSIRO it became CSIRO appropriation and therefore the exemption did not apply (in its intent – the funding was not a grant or sponsorship rather a payment for services delivered by CSIRO as the partner in the project)
  • the business unit could not adequately demonstrate that the use of one provider met any exemption or limited tender condition, due to limited market research
  • the CSIRO, as Australia’s innovation catalysts, should be taking every opportunity to engage with industry to encourage competition

As a result, the limited tender/exemption request was not approved, and an open tender was recommended.

The sourcing team worked with the GAB project team to develop scope, outcomes and tender documentation, which was released to the market via an open tender.

The team was pleasantly surprised when this competitive approach generated several compelling responses, including the initially identified supplier offering an alternative bid. This bid outlined a new to market solution that would allow underwater exploration to beyond 3000m below sea level. This had never been done before, however, the solution would take several months to fully develop.

Following the evaluation process, however, another bid demonstrated a better value for money solution.  The alternate bid submitted by the initially identified potential supplier could not be utilised because of compelling time constraints around the need to enter the GAB in a small window of time. This did not allow development of the new solution.

The main hiccups were demonstrating to the business unit that conducting appropriate early market research and going to open market were not tools to make their lives difficult but that can be used to actively engage with the market to encourage innovation in the sector and discover solutions that were previously unknown.

In debriefing with the business unit, they noted how excited they were to receive the alternate bid but how disappointed they were as they were not able to use it due to the time constraints.

Had they conducted the early market research prior to formulating a view of the market and spending valuable time pursuing that view, they may have had opportunity to utilise the new technology within their timeframe.

Engaging early with the Strategic Procurement Team would also have greatly assisted the team in navigating the sourcing landscape and understanding the rich opportunities available when seeking innovative solutions to problems.

As a result of the experience, the team advised they would be engaging with the Strategic Procurement Team in the early stages of planning their projects so they can better engage with industry in the future.

Here are some top tips from the experience:

  • engaging early with the internal sourcing teams is strategically beneficial to the organisation and supports a value for money outcome
  • early engagement with industry promotes innovation and allows scope for new to market solutions
  • a fully functional strategic sourcing team is an enabler rather than a blocker and by effectively navigating the resource management framework, the Australian government can produce innovation through early market research and engagement, and via open approaches to market

Digital Messaging Network -
Queensland’s Department of
Transport and Main Roads

What was the project?

We had an idea to develop a digital messaging network in Queensland that would provide customer updates, information and advertising.  It would be free to the department and could possibly even generate some revenue.

Was it a large project?

We were unsure of the size in the early stages — We were thinking of doing an initial phase which focused on Gold Coast light rail and a few of major motorways in South East Queensland. Then later, expanding the scope to the whole state of Queensland (roads and buses).

Why did you do early market research?

We had a vague idea around the sort of technology which was available and the scope of what we wanted.  However, we didn’t know if there were other technologies and we wanted to explore further. We also wanted to find out who the main suppliers were in the market and better understand each technology. All this information would then inform the Department’s decision as to the direction we would take with the available technology, including whether or not to even conduct a procurement — And if we were to procure, which type of procurement method and how best to approach the market.

How were sellers researched/located?

There were some existing sellers that we were aware of (major advertising companies in Brisbane) and these were invited directly to participate. To cast a wider net, we also published an early market research Brief on ‘QTenders’ and Commonwealth Digital Marketplace websites.

How were sellers contacted?

We asked sellers for written submissions in response to the Brief. We treated the early market research process as per normal procurement and probity guidelines — With fairness and transparency.

We did the evaluation step in two stages:

  1. First, we shortlisted written submissions.
  2. Second, we had face-to face meetings with shortlisted companies and evaluated again (using a weighted method — where each criteria in the evaluation report has a different weight, and we marked the degree to which each seller met each criteria).

How did you mitigate probity risks?

An independent probity advisor was hired, and they participated in the process from the onset, where they reviewed the early market research Brief, to the very end when they produced a Probity Report confirming all probity principles were upheld.

How was it all documented?

We documented the research using the following Department templates:

  • Written Brief (all supporting approval documents in the background)
  • Evaluation sheets and Evaluation Reports for both stages
  • Meeting minutes
  • Final Evaluation Report and Recommendation (as to next steps)
  • Probity Report

What were the outcomes?

We formulated recommendations about what to do next.

There were two companies that could do what we wanted and they were ready to start implementing a solution within the Department’s timeline.

Some other sellers proposed fairly risky approaches, and it was recommended to further engage with these sellers better understand the risks.

The next step was to develop a budget bid to get funding to enable the next procurement step.  The market research was invaluable in formulating a picture of the potential costs involved in the solution we were seeking and shaping the formal approach to market.

We also discovered that Queensland Rail were conducting their own project with similar goals.

Were there any hiccups along the way?

One challenge we had along the way was some resistance from the probity advisor. They took a very compliance-based approach and provided advice on ways in which we could not do things. Perhaps a more constructive approach would have been to also provide feedback on navigating the probity concerns while reaching a compliant, timely outcome.

Were there any lessons we could learn from?

We found the most value in terms of time, responsiveness and information gathered was to prioritise in person meetings, rather than just receiving written submissions. In face-to-face meetings, valuable insights emerged that we wouldn’t have otherwise obtained. Suppliers will often send written presentations or documentation following a face-to-face meeting so you have access to the important information after the meetings.

Any ‘top tips’ for others who may go through this process?

My top tip is to focus on encouraging and empowering internal resources to focus on a timely, compliant outcome.  To think of innovative approaches, which remain compliant while also achieving your desired outcomes.  Another tip is to actively engage with the risks – Identifying and treating them.  Being able to articulate the risks of an early market research activity will assist with probity concerns and provide defensible evidence should issues arise.

Anything else to add?

We’re now doing another early market research process for a 5G deployment project. It is in the initial phases and we are using the same templates and processes. As with the case study, this approach is expected to uncover new technologies and solutions to better inform the formal approach to market

Upgrade of a Financial
Management Accounting System -
<Who was the Department?>

What was the project?

The project was to find a solution that fit within the project’s aim of improving accounting processing functions and providing a stable and reliable accounting system, while ensuring that costs were within the allocated capital budget.

This was an ‘Outcomes-based’ requirements brief, and at the early market research phase, this is the best approach to allow for innovative proposals, lead by the expertise in the market.

Was it a large project?

It was a small project of approximately $110,000.

Why did you do early market research?

Early market research was conducted to communicate the entity’s needs, like product functionalities and cost.

We found that there were only two sellers who may be able to meet our needs — let’s call them Seller A and Seller B.

Seller A was the incumbent supplier and Seller B was identified through desktop research of available information (websites, marketing materials, etc).

Following assessment, Seller B was set aside from further consideration as it was clear their offerings would be very cost prohibitive and would not provide a value for money outcome for the need. While certainly not the only factor in assessing value for money, total costs against budget expectations is a consideration.

Once we identified the seller who best met the project requirements, a Business case was prepared for the project.

In summary:  In line with the identified risks and the small scale of the activity, following the desktop research and a value for money assessment, the agency went with the existing seller (Seller A) to continue to provide and uplift services.  This resulted in a cost and time effective outcome.

Some sellers often invite current and potential clients to a networking session and a number of agencies attend the event. We can attend without fear of probity concerns, as it was outlined from the very beginning that the session was held as a knowledge sharing activity, with no commercial implications for all systems administrators, while allowing the seller to showcase their new products and updates to the system, such as bug fixes, new tools, etc.

If there is complete transparency from both you as the agency, and the seller, about meetings and workshops, then probity fears shouldn’t stop you from exploring ICT options for your project.

Internal Network for a Navy Project
– Department of Defence

What was the project?

We did some early market research in support of a Defence project, specifically for the Navy. The project was to establish an internal network based on a Commercial-of-the-Shelf (COTS) product. A small team worked together to define the scope of the project and estimate what components were required.

These were then tested during the market research activity.

Was it a large project?

It was a large project of approximately $1 million.

What were the goals of your early market research?

  • To obtain a rough estimate of price for budgeting purposes
  • To better understand delivery times
  • To gauge the market’s interest and ability to deliver the solution

How were sellers researched/located?

Potential sellers were researched on the internet. There were numerous domestic suppliers that could deliver the components required.

How were sellers contacted/how was the market research accomplished?

Sellers were initially contacted by email and those that showed interest (i.e. provided information in email responses) were then contacted by phone and asked some clarification questions (around availability of parts, prices and delivery times).

How did you ensure sellers were treated equally?

All sellers were told the project was in an early market research phase and that it may or may not form a future procurement. Therefore, details of a formal tender process could not be provided.

No promises of work were made, and it was also made clear that the project scope was still being defined.

How was it documented?

The research was captured in a project budget/cost model supported by prices and delivery times that had been provided. Records of conversations were not kept, just email correspondences, evaluations and outcomes.

What were the outcomes?

The project was not approved as other options were pursued. However, the objectives of the early market research were met and we had a much clearer understanding of the capability and capacity in the market to meet the need.

Were there any hiccups along the way?

One seller ended up sending verbose and excessive promotional material, even after the market research activity was complete.

Some Sellers that followed up with emails and phone calls were advised that the project was not approved. However, some sellers kept contacting Defence to find out whether there were any other upcoming tenders.

Were there any lessons we could learn from?

In hind sight, it may have been good to make it clear up-front that only requested information for the allocated period would be accepted, and that excessive or irrelevant promotional material would be disregarded.

Any ‘top tips’ that can be taken from the case study?

More meaningful market research can be undertaken if the project’s scope and requirements are better defined. Sellers would ask questions where Defence’s answer was often “that hasn’t been decided yet” or “Defence is still considering its options.”
