Better Practice Corner

Navigating the process of early market engagement can be daunting, but don’t worry, we’re here to help you. Below are some topics with FAQs to guide you along the process, but if you have any further questions, contact your procurement department for help and support.

Understanding and preparation

Click each FAQ to expand it and learn more.

  • Early market research is a clear two-way communication between government agencies and industry, with a goal of:

    • gathering information to have a broader and deeper view of the capability of industry and sellers/suppliers
    • considering market intelligence in relation to the aims of a larger program, before any individual programs commence
    • getting a better understanding of industry trends as well as new and innovative solutions to our challenges
    • moving outside a panel to expand our outcomes
    • finding possible better matches between our needs in government and industry’s capabilities in meeting those needs
    • encouraging new vendors to emerge and finding potential vendors for future work
    • aligning our sourcing strategies with the market
    • ‘trying before we buy’ so when the procurement process comes, we are informed buyers
    • helping the market make the most of government business opportunities
    • better planning to mitigate risks 

    Note that doing early market research does not lock you into any vendor or any specific digital sourcing solution; it is just a first step to seeing what’s out there, exploring possibilities, and realistically gauging the capacity for the ICT industry to contribute to the program. 

    Early market research involves pre-procurement activities and so is separate from any actual digital sourcing processes.

  • A rapidly changing landscape
    Technology is evolving at an increasingly fast pace, and the ICT industry is a rapidly changing landscape. When we work on large-scale or high-investment ICT projects, it’s important that we speak to people and companies who work in the area we’re interested in, to make sure we are getting the most up-to-date information to make informed decisions about how projects will be carried out and to be informed buyers when choosing vendors.

    Be more strategic
    By engaging with the ICT industry at an early stage, we can be more strategic and systematic in our dealings with vendors and the industry, rather than just engaging with them on a more short-term, program-by-program basis. This benefits whole programs and not just individual projects.

    Align requirements with the ICT industry
    A key strategic aspect of early market research is that it can help agencies express desired outcomes and translate them into high-level statements of business requirements that are well aligned with the ICT industry, making it much more likely that the desired outcomes can be achieved. Therefore, early market research is an important dialogue between your agency and the ICT industry aimed at resolving the content of your requirement.

    Invite innovation
    If we are flexible over the content of our requirement, then we can offer the ICT industry the opportunity to propose previously unforeseen and possibly innovative solutions.

    Conversely, if it turns out that there isn’t an industry solution to our brief, then we will need to revisit our requirements and scope, and we can do this with minimal cost and effort if we have done our market research early.

    Rich data and deeper understandings
    Early market research allows you to access rich sources of data and information from industry on the way a particular market operates, the types of services and products available, industry pricing structures and any future changes to the industry. It also gives us a deeper understanding of a particular category of goods or services — leading to the identification of initiatives aligned to business value drivers and value-for-money outcomes.

    Robust documentation
    This early process supports the development of robust ‘request’ documentation relevant to the targeted industry, reducing the need for addenda or post-tender clarifications later on.

  • Not all programs need early market research, but if you can answer ‘yes’ to any one of these questions, then you should consider it:

    1. Is there doubt over the existence of a market for the identified business need or product?
    2. Assuming there is a market of some kind, are there doubts over its capacity, capability, maturity or competitiveness?
    3. Is first-hand in-house agency knowledge of the market superficial, incomplete or simply absent?
    4. Is there uncertainty about the level of suppliers' interest?
    5. Is the desired outcome likely to involve significant business change within your agency?
    6. Is the level of expected business change within the agency exceptional?
    7. Is there a need to manage expectations of the program with suppliers?
    8. Is your agency requirement unusual or novel?
    9. Is the solution to your requirement likely to be complex or innovative?

    Early market research is not appropriate for every program, but where it is, it should begin at the earliest possible stage in the digital sourcing process to assess the reaction of the market, the proposal required, and the digital sourcing approach. 

  • Your agency should not undertake early market research if:

    • the agency has an existing standing offer arrangement (SOA) in place that continues to offer value for money
    • there are other sensitivities around the program in question (e.g. security or political).

  • The panels are a great resource for early market research, especially with panels that are all encompassing, for example, the ‘Ask the Market’ option in the Digital Market Place can reach over 1200 sellers, so panels should certainly be considered.

    However, panels do rule out emerging or new vendors, or smaller enterprises who haven’t applied to be on a panel yet. If we are to invite innovative solutions, then doing early market research both with panels, and outside of panels, will give us the best options to find something that suits our needs, offers value for money and can future-proof projects with the most up-to-date solutions and technology.

    • By talking to industry and market vendors early on in the digital sourcing processes, we give them time to meet the future demands of government.
    • It provides an opportunity for industry to be informed about government digital sourcing processes, which is particularly relevant in sectors where suppliers may lack experience in completing government tenders.
    • We don’t know what we don’t know! By going out to market, vendors are able to better inform us of the industry capabilities, emerging technologies and new solutions. It’s a great way to share information.
    • Complex, innovative or high-risk programs can be adapted to better utilise ICT industry capacities and so vendors are in a better position to meet the needs of such programs.
    • Early market research can help shape the requirement so that digital sourcing is focused on what the market can deliver, which again puts vendors in better positions to meet government program needs.
    • Through this research, we also educate the market and confront any preconceptions regarding transacting with agencies.
    • Early market research is outcomes focussed — that means we acknowledge that we are not the experts, and we let the market tell us what’s possible instead of working in a vacuum.

    For more information on early market research and vendors, check out our Case Studies section.

Conducting early market research

Click each FAQ to expand it and learn more.

  • Not necessarily.

    Early market research can involve more than the existing sellers you have on your panel, as the idea is to discover new trends, new technologies, innovation and new vendors, including international offerings. That is not to say, however, that existing suppliers can’t offer you services in a new way or in a new area, so it’s likely that you will involve existing suppliers, but there is no rule that they have to be involved and consulted.

  • A tip to get you started is to always approach the open market in your early market research as this avoids missing out on those vendors you don’t even know about — you might think you know the market and who the leading vendors are but this is not necessarily the case. There may be companies that are just starting, with a huge potential, that you have never heard about.

    You can find information about potential vendors from these sources:

    • You can always start with Google to get a general idea of who the players in the market are
    • Look for vendor names on AusTender (or a state government equivalent, for example, QTenders, NSW eTenders, etc.), then go to their website for more information
    • Gartner
    • Commonwealth Digital Marketplace website
    • Agency records and documents
    • IBISWorld report (if you have a subscription)
    • Talk to stakeholders and other people that have run tenders for similar products/services

    There may be an internal approval process before you start making phone calls, or sending out a Brief, so be sure to check your agency’s internal policy or have a chat with your procurement team. 

    After receiving submissions from the open market, shortlist vendors you really want to talk to. This is done based on some pre-determined criteria that will be specific to the digital sourcing project, so check with your team and the procurement department if you are uncertain. You may like to use targeted emails, or phone conversations to shortlist vendors.

  • Much of your early market research may just be just that — research — or you may also find it useful to contact and connect with vendors. Here are some ideas on how you can engage with vendors in your research:

    Engagement:

    Much of this early engagement will be informal, and should involve a gradual learning journey that progressively builds knowledge from your research phase:

    • Attendance at industry conferences and trade shows
    • Supplier and customer visits/tours
    • Product demonstrations and site/facilities inspections
    • Inviting industry to participate in working groups
    • Industry briefings or ‘consultation days’ or ‘technology days’
    • Engagement through any industry forums or groups
    • Engagement of consultants, advisers or other industry experts
    • Use of industry stakeholder or working groups to review and provide feedback on potential solutions, options or requirements

  • Consultants offer specialist experience and knowledge that you or your agency may not currently possess, so you may want to involve a consultant in anything from offering advice to managing the whole early market research process.

    Here are some things to consider when using consultants for this early stage of industry research:

    • Consultants can only advise you on what they see as the best options, based on the information they hold and what they are told by the agency and the suppliers.
    • The business risk of the program as a whole remains with the agency commissioning the consultant.
    • Consultants who offer advice, or who manage the process on behalf of the agency, must be impartial.
    • You and your staff must learn from consultants to avoid reliance upon them.
    • Engage with the supplier base as early as possible. Before consultation happens, make sure you do research to establish whether any new providers have emerged since any previous digital sourcing or market research were undertaken. Consulting with the broadest range of suppliers possible helps ensure that a range of different perspectives are considered.
    • Discussions should centre on the ‘ends’ rather than the ‘means’ (define the requirement not the solution). Gather information rather than providing details of the desired solution, and try and stay focused on the desired outcomes, while the ICT suppliers explain how they could help achieve those outcomes.
    • The most common issue is usually a lack of planning. This leads to prioritising schedule above all else and then specifying a solution to save time.
    • Keep options open and avoid the tendency to 'zero in' on particular options — allow suppliers the freedom to suggest ways forward.
    • Make sure vendors know you are in early market research phase and don’t guarantee work.
    • It is important to be as informed as possible about the market before commencing the engagement. This will make it possible for the agency to determine what they do and don’t know, and the subsequent market research can be used to fill in the knowledge gaps.
    • Meeting with supplier representatives in person is the most straightforward, and often the most successful method of engaging with industry. Individual meetings with suppliers often lead to more useful information being elicited than group briefings, as supplier representatives are likely to be reluctant to share information when competitors are present.
    • Make sure your communication with the supplier base is as uniform as possible. You should ensure that all identified suppliers are given the same level of access to digital sourcing staff and the same level of opportunity to contribute to the engagement process.
    • You can discuss previous digital sourcing for the same products/services or in the same market space. This will assist suppliers in providing relevant information and insights. However, be mindful of not disclosing commercial-in-confidence information, or getting side-tracked into a discussion on past contracts, rather than future requirements.
    • Sound recordkeeping is vital to document that an agency’s probity and accountability obligations have been adhered to. Records of your early market research should include who the agency has engaged with, the reason for the engagement, and any actions resulting from the engagement.

  • After you have shortlisted your vendors, you should end up with an ‘evaluation outcome’ document of some sort; it can be an Evaluation Report, or just a list of vendors shortlisted in an Excel sheet (see the Documents & Templates section for options).

    Make sure you document:

    • your decision-making process and application of the assessment criteria
    • who ran the shortlisting
    • the final list of vendors, with their contact details

    There is also a need to justify the reasons for NOT shortlisting potential vendors (possible complaints, situations when a supplier contacts you later and asks why they were not invited, etc.).

  • It’s a good idea to publish a Brief that:

    • states the problem
    • says what you are looking for
    • sets out pre-determined criteria for the solution (this can be adapted as you progress through your research)
    • says how you are going to engage with the market
    • details how you are going to shortlist vendors

    In general, be honest and don’t mislead suppliers. If your department is not going to do anything after the early market research (apart from issuing an internal report), tell this to vendors; they will then understand that there is no sales opportunity (and it will help them to stop pestering you).

    Note that you should treat all vendors the same in your early market research, as any differences in treatment would contravene the fairness and transparency principles that underline public digital sourcing.

  • Proper planning is necessary if early market research is to result in the desired outcome.

    Poorly planned early market research can result in inadequate outcomes for both your agency and industry, as well as unjustified costs in terms of time spent and resources allocated to the engagement. Furthermore, your agency could suffer from reputational damage if industry feels that the early market research process was poorly facilitated.

    There is no ‘one size fits all’ approach to planning an early market research process. It is important to understand what information you want to share with industry, what information is required from industry, and what questions need to be raised, before initiating contact. This will help ensure that the agency drives the direction of the engagement.

    If industry sets the parameters of the discussion, there is a risk that the information attained is akin to a ‘sales pitch’, which may not be beneficial in terms of broadening the knowledge of industry and clarifying requirements.

  • Risks associated with a lack of early market research include:

    • poorly designed specifications
    • unsuitable conditions of contract
    • a lack of tender responses
    • inferior value-for-money outcomes

  • The early market research process itself carries little risk to you or your agency if these principles are followed:

    • Uniformity: while no two suppliers are alike, engagement with all suppliers consulted should be as uniform and consistent as possible.
    • Security: if information is provided by a supplier, it should not be shared with other suppliers (directly or inadvertently) or people outside of your agency unless permission from the supplier is attained. Any documentation provided by suppliers should be stored securely.
    • Boundaries: you and your staff should be open with suppliers about what can and cannot be discussed. The purpose of the early market research must be clearly communicated to suppliers in the Brief to avoid any misunderstanding.

  • Early market research is considered to be a planning activity, therefore, the process should cease when the your agency has understood what it is that is required and you are prepared to proceed to Approach to Market (or have determined that the digital sourcing process will not proceed).

    Communications to suppliers about the end of the early market research period should be made at the same time, to ensure that no supplier is unfairly advantaged.

    It is important to note that ending the early market research period should not inhibit standard ‘business as usual’ with existing suppliers. However, once the digital sourcing becomes a competitive process, any discussion that could potentially advantage (whether real or perceived) one Respondent over another should not occur.

Probity

Click each FAQ to expand it and learn more.

  • Probity is the complete and confirmed integrity, uprightness and honesty in a digital sourcing or pre-digital sourcing process. In short, it is the evidence of ethical behaviour.

    As government agencies, we have an obligation to ensure our digital sourcing conduct is always fair, ethical, transparent and probity-rich. If we have clear, visible and meaningful commitments to fairness, then it encourages suppliers to want to do business with us now and in the future.

  • Probity risks in early market engagement include:

    • not treating vendors equitably
    • claims of bias
    • claims of improper use of position
    • accepting hospitality, gifts of benefits from suppliers
    • conflicts of interest
    • excluding suppliers for inconsequential reasons

    Whatever method is chosen to undertake early market research, it is critical that there is no element of bid selection, evaluation, actual or perceived conflict of interest, commitment or undertaking towards any potential supplier.

    Where information is provided by a supplier, and an agency wishes to use that information, careful consideration must be given to potentially confidential information, or use of the supplier’s intellectual property.

  • It is important to note that the obligations of probity and accountability should in no way stop an agency from undertaking early market research and engaging in reasonable discussion with potential suppliers for a new digital sourcing requirement. Rather, early market research gives us an opportunity to demonstrate to industry that we are undertaking due diligence and consultation before presenting a Request to the market.

    Probity decisions should be:

    • Helpful: Probity should be used to facilitate discussion of current approaches to market with suppliers in order to promote genuine engagement.
    • Inclusive: Probity processes should be designed to enable innovative approaches to be adopted if required.
    • Tailored: Probity roles can be tailored to the business need and range from using internal expertise through to engaging external specialist advice.
    • Sensible: Each individual process may require a different approach; one size will not fit all.

    Read over the case studies and blog posts to find out more about how you can show evidence of ethical behaviour in your research.  

  • It’s often thought that meeting vendors for an informal chat over coffee might be cause probity issues, but as long as you tell the vendor you will report the meeting to your manager (and you follow through with that) so there is a record of the meeting, you’ll have protected yourself and the research. Also ensure you reiterate that you are in early market research phase and don’t guarantee work whatsoever.

    Note that receiving unsolicited calls are also fine, you just need to document the outcome of the call as a file note.
